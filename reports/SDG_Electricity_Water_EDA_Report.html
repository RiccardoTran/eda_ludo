1. Overview

What This Report Is About

This report examines two essential services in Sub-Saharan Africa:

  • Electricity - Do people have power in their homes?
  • Clean Water - Do people have access to safe drinking water?

We compare urban areas (cities, towns) with rural areas (villages, countryside) to understand the access gaps.

6. Comparative Analysis

Urban vs Rural Gap

The Urban-Rural Divide

In both electricity and water, cities consistently have better access than villages. This is called the "urban-rural gap."

Key Comparisons

  • Electricity gap is larger than water gap: Villages are further behind in electricity access than in water access
  • Urban areas lead in both services: Cities have significantly better infrastructure for both electricity and water
  • Water access is higher overall: Both urban and rural water access rates are higher than their electricity counterparts
  • Rural electricity is the biggest challenge: With only ~35% average access, rural electrification remains a major development priority

7. Conclusions

Summary in Plain Language

This analysis shows that access to electricity and water in Sub-Saharan Africa varies greatly depending on where you live:

  • If you live in a city - You likely have access to both electricity (~80%) and water (~85%)
  • If you live in a village - Water access is moderate (~55%) but electricity access is low (~35%)

Main Findings

1. The Urban-Rural Gap Persists

Cities consistently outperform villages in both electricity and water access. This gap is larger for electricity than for water.

2. Progress is Being Made

All four indicators show improvement over time. Both urban and rural areas are seeing increased access to these essential services.

3. Rural Electricity is the Biggest Challenge

With only about 35% average access, rural electrification remains the area with the most room for improvement.

4. Country Variation is Significant

There are large differences between countries. Some have achieved near-universal access, while others still have very low coverage.

Why This Matters

  • Health: Without electricity, clinics cannot refrigerate vaccines. Without clean water, waterborne diseases spread.
  • Education: Schools need electricity for lights and computers. Children who collect water miss school hours.
  • Economic Development: Businesses need reliable power and water to operate.
  • Gender Equality: Women and girls disproportionately bear the burden of collecting water and managing without electricity.

Data Limitations

  • Data shows percentage access only, not quality of service
  • Some countries have missing data for certain years
  • National averages may hide regional differences within countries
  • Different data sources (World Bank, JMP) may use different methodologies
